ABSTRACT

This thesis examines various advancements in the field of automated aesthet- ics assessment and presents improvements that delivers state of the art performance in

the task. For comparison, a reference model is needed. To go about building such a

model, we first establish two baselines, one which uses handcrafted features, and an- other which relies on deep learning methods. The handcrafted baseline uses a Support

Vector Machine ( SVM ) for classification, whereas for the deep learning baseline we

instead perform transfer learning on an existing convolutional neural network model

pre-trained on ImageNet. To improve upon the baseline, we propose three additions:

incorporating textual features extracted from comments, the use of multiple Global

Activation Pooling ( GAP ) layers, and performing 10-way classification prediction.

We extracted textual features via a two layer Recurrent Neural Network ( RNN ). The

inclusion of GAP layers allows us to visualize class specific activation maps, which

provides information regarding which part of the image the model likes and dislikes.

We also perform probability approximation using 10-way classification with kullback- libler loss. The inclusion of the improvements mentioned above delivers state of the

art performance, with a classification accuracy of 82.27%. We also built three applica- tions to showcase the practicality of the research, a webservice where users can upload

images to be evaluated via a API, an Android gallery app that rates images on mo- bile devices, and a web game where users can test their skills at aesthetics assessment

against state of the art deep learning models.

CHAPTER 1

INTRODUCTION

In the past decade, large improvements have been made in the area of digital storage,

performance, and battery life. We can now store hundreds or even thousands of images

in a chip smaller than a fingernail, which we use to keep images taken from our very

capable camera that also doubles as our phone. Unfortunately, with the ever increasing

amount of images taken everyday, one may find it ever harder to stick out against the

crowd.

The ability to discern automatically whether an image is aesthetically pleasing

is an interesting problem that has many applications. The benefit of a system able to do

so would be two fold: the system could make helpful tips and suggestions to improve

our skills as photographers and it could help to filter images that are below a certain

threshold of aesthetics. This can also be applied to cull photos from phone storage by

providing recommendations on ugly images to delete.

Computational aesthetics assessment also seeks to challenge a domain that we

commonly view as a purely human endeavor. One of the challenges faced by research

in this area is that the problem is innately subjective, as whether an image is aesthetic

varies from person to person. In its basest form, aesthetics can be summarized as the

appreciation of beauty. Generally, an image that is pleasing to the eye is considered

1

Страница 16 из 96

aesthetic, however there exists no single consensus as to what exactly constitutes an

aesthetic image. To work around this problem, we elect to use a large quantity of

data, particularly the AVA dataset, a standardized collection of over 200 000 images

that draws its source from the website dpchallenge.com. dpchallenge.com is a popular

site where professional photographers gather to rate each others images. With a large

supply of information regarding what we think of as aesthetics and not, it has been

shown that this equalizes the effects of individual bias. Notably, one issue with this

approach is that this may not be reflective of the aesthetic tastes of the layman, as the

refined taste of professional photographers may spot issues with photos that may be

ignored or even liked by the common Joe.

Despite the ambiguity of the situation, previous research has shown that there

exists certain rules or visual properties that can be used to provide a reasonably accu- rate estimate of aesthetic quality (Datta, Joshi, Li, & Wang, 2006). Features detection

algorithms such as Scale Invariant Feature Transform(SIFT) can be used to detect local

features within images, and with a classifier such as the Support vector machine(SVM)

are able to generalize relatively well to a wide array of images (Marchesotti, Perronnin,

Larlus, & Csurka, 2011).

Ever since Alex Krizhevsky won the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition

Competition (ILSVRC) 2012 with his convolutional neural network (CNN) model,

Alexnet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012a), deep learning has taken the world by storm. In

computer vision, CNNs have been particularly effective, and state of the art perfor- mance in most tasks are now held by variants of the CNN architecture. In fact, even

2

Страница 17 из 96

out of the box, popular image classification deep models were shown to have great

results in aesthetics assessment, easily beating previous best efforts with handcrafted

solutions even without any fine-tuning.

1.1 Problem Statement

The primary problem this project intends to solve is the question: what makes

an image aesthetically pleasing? and the follow up question: how do I efficiently

determine that computationally?

With the large increase of images now available online, it is quite impractical

for any one person to view every single image manually and leave a rating. Frequently,

excellent images are buried, whether they be posted simply at the wrong place, the

wrong time, or a combination of both. Indeed, the artistic genius that only received

his due recognition long after his death is not exactly rare. A number of great artists

died poor and destitute, only to be hailed as a genius decades or even centuries later.

A fair and unbiased image aesthetic assessment model will allow true masterpieces to

receive their due recognition.

The project also aims to solve photo bloat in user galleries. With just about

every individual who owns a smart phone having a capable camera in their pocket, the

amount of images being taken daily has grown exponentially. Normally, the users who

face storage issues would have to resort to manually sifting through their photo album

and deleting images. With a system that is capable of automatically rate images from

the individuals galleries, they would simply have to check the proposed deletes of low

quality images detected by the system and give the go-ahead.

Finally, the project aims to improve the accuracy of image aesthetics classifica- tion. To do that, we will evaluate past methods and strategies for performing aesthetics

assessment and improve upon them.

1.2 Objectives

The objectives of this thesis are:

• To design and implement a better model, improving upon existing deep learning

architectures to increase classification accuracy.

• To explore the inclusion of textual features and its impact on aesthetics classifi- cation performance.

• To develop real world applications using the results of this thesis to showcase

the practicality of research in this area.

1.3 Scope

In this project, the primary objective is to build a performant and accurate aes- thetics classifier for the images using the AVA ( A Large-Scale Database for Aesthetic

Visual Analysis ) dataset as benchmark. The AVA database is the reference data set for

almost all aesthetics related research, and therefore, will be used in this project.

LITERATURE REVIEW

This chapter covers existing work in this area. We will discuss in sequence the data sets

available for aesthetics research, classical methods using handcrafted image features

and finally deep learning methods, up to and including the state of the art solutions.

2.1 Image Aesthetics Data Sets

There have been many data sets that have emerged and subsequently faded into

obscurity over the years as better and more comprehensive data sets were introduced.

The earliest papers simply rolled their own data sets by downloading from public im- age sharing sites like Photo.net like in the paper by Datta et al.(2006).

2.1.1 CUHK-PQ

The dataset introduced by Ke, Tang, and Jing (n.d.) contains over 12,000 im- ages, half of which are considered low quality and the rest high quality. Images were

derived from dpchallenge.com, and fall into just two classes, good or bad. CUHK-PQ

only provides images with very clear rater’s consensus on their scores, which made the

classification task eventually too easy, with many methods achieving accuracy scores

above 90%.

2.1.2 MIRFLICKR/ Image CLEF: Visual Concept Detection and Annotation

Task 2011

MIRFLICKR is a large dataset that contains over 1 million images crawled

from Flickr, along with textual tags, aesthetic annotations and EXIF meta-data.

2.1.3 AVA (A Large Scale Database for Aesthetic Visual Analysis)

The AVA database introduced by Murray, Marchesotti, and Perronnin (2012)

was the first large database of richly annotated images for computational aesthetics.

It had a rather unique characteristic where images were scored in response to photo- graphic challenges. This leads to some ambiguity as a person’s response to an image is

quite likely to be affected by the challenge an image is submitted to, but it would very

difficult for a computer to determine that. Luckily, the large number of images seems

to negate that problem somewhat. In the paper, a model was proposed and trained with

a Support Vector Machine (SVM) with fisher vector signatures generated from both

SIFT and color descriptors. The model obtained a maximum of 67% accuracy. The

AVA dataset includes three types of annotations: aesthetic, semantic and photographic

style annotations. Offering many advantages over its peers at the time of its release,

it has become the de-facto standard for image aesthetics evaluation. As of the time

of writing, the AVA dataset still remains the most popular dataset, with most recent

published papers still using it for benchmarking. A simple summary of features the

AVA dataset offered above others at the time can be seen in Figure 2.1.

2.2 Handcrafted Methods

In the early days, hand-crafted visual descriptors was used to evaluate photos

and other images by mimicking rules of thumb used by photographers. Using intuition

and photographic rules used by professionals, visual features were extracted so that

they may be used to discriminate between visually pleasing and displeasing images.

2.2.1 Photographic Rules

In the paper by Datta et al. (2006), using a features chosen based on photo- graphic rules, intuition, and observed trends in ratings. 56 visual features were ex- tracted per image, and were then trained by SVM, achieving an accuracy of 59.6%.

The dataset used was a sample of images from the large online photo sharing

community Photo.Net. Images were peer-rated in terms of two qualities, aesthetics and

originality. Scores are given in a range from 0 - 7, with 0 being the worst and 7 being

the best. This paper observes that the correlation between originality and aesthetics

scores were very strong, with just 1.1% having disparity of more than 1.0 between

average aesthetics and average originality. For this reason, the paper chose to only use

the aesthetics value of images. Although groundbreaking at the time, the method is

used is today relatively computationally expensive, and does not scale well to larger

data sets.

Familiarity measures were also used as a feature. Novelty was observed to

have a significant influence on our perception of the aesthetics of a picture. In order to

capture this factor of human judgment, the paper defines a new measure of familiarity

based on the integrated region matching ( IRM ) image distance. The IRM distance

properties of photographic images and their aesthetic ratings, marking a significant

step in the challenging task of determining the aesthetics value of an image computa- tionally.

2.2.2 Generic Descriptors

In the landmark paper by Marchesotti et al. (2011), the use of generic image

descriptors was proposed. Instead of trying to model best practices explicitly, they

instead opted to do so implicitly. Using task-independent descriptors, they were able

to comfortably outperform then state of the art features.

Their pipeline starts with either a SIFT ( Scale-invariant feature transform ) or

11

Страница 26 из 96

color descriptors, which have their dimensions reduced via PCA. The statistics of the

distribution of these features are then encoded using a fisher vector up to the second

order. Fisher vectors are global image features obtained by pooling visual features.

Finally, a linear SVM with hinge loss using the primal formulation and a

Stochastic Gradient Descent(SGD) algorithm is trained with the encoded visual fea- tures as input.

2.3 Deep Learning Approaches

Convolutional neural networks (CNN) have been shown to be extremely effec- tive in a wide range of applications, and image aesthetics is no exception. Instead of

using generic descriptors, the network learns suitable representations from raw pixels

in the input RGB images alone. Even without any data preprocessing beyond resiz- ing into a standard resolution, CNNs have been shown to produce significantly better

classification performance than prior methods. The main catch however is that for best

performance, CNN requires a large amount of input data to train.

Modern approaches involving deep learning in aesthetics assessment have ob- vious roots in object recognition. Therefore, it seems appropriate to first discuss ad- vancements in object recognition, followed by how those advancements have been

subsequently applied in the area of image aesthetics.

2.3.3 Composition Preservation

One significant limitation with current convolutional neural networks is that all

inputs must conform to the same dimensions, due to the use of pooling layers. As a

result, images were usually cropped or warped to fit a specific dimension, thus causing

a loss of information and possibly confusing the network.

The paper by (Mai et al., 2016) proposes Multi-Net Spatial Pooling Convo- lutional Neural Network ( MNA-CNN ), which makes use of an adaptive spatial

pooling layer, as shown in Figure 2.8. The network is inspired by the success of SPP- Net (He, Zhang, Ren, & Sun, 2014) for visual recognition, and brings two important

improvements. The model allows arbitrary size input to be used in both training and

testing and it makes use of multiple sub networks for different pooling sizes instead of

a spatial pyramid pooling layer.

Additionally, to allow for multi-scale feature extraction, multi-net adaptive spa- tial pooling convnet architecture is used to effectively combine outputs from multiple

subnetworks with different spatial pooling sizes. The overall architecture of the net- work can be seen in Figure 2.8 and Figure 2.9.

The MNA-CNN consists multiple sub-networks, each of which is a copy of

the base network with the last pooling layer replaced with an adaptive pooling layer.

The same input image is shared by all networks, and their output is then combined

and averaged to obtain the overall prediction. The networks were trained separate to

3.1 Handcrafted Methods

3.1.1 Feature Extraction

Feature extraction is a common first step for image classification. The method

used has a significant impact on the performance of the model, and thus must be care- fully selected. The popular Scale Invariant Feature Transform ( SIFT ) (Lowe, 2004)

will be used for the baseline of this thesis.

3.1.2 Scale Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT)

SIFT was introduced by Lowe (2004), and is probably the most popular clas- sical feature extractor. The steps taken in SIFT can be divided into 4 major stages:

the detection of scale-space extrema, keypoint localization,orientation assignment and

keypoint descriptor.

The first stage of SIFT, detection of scale-space extrema, searches for interest

points that are invariant to scale and rotation by using a difference-of-Gradient func- tion. The central idea is that is that objects are only meaningful at a certain scale.

To create a scale space, an image is progressively blurred. For SIFT, several octaves

of the image is first generated. Each octave doubles the size of the image and ap- ply anti-aliasing(blur). For each octave, substraction is done, as shown in Figure 3.1,

to generate Difference of Gaussians (DoG) of different sizes. The following step is
